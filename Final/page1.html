Chitra Banerjee and Her Writings

Chitra Banerjee’s interpretation of Indian mythological epics gives a new life to the ancient epic, offering a female point of view that is both introspective and revolutionary. The Palace of Illusions is the retelling of Mahabharata from Draupadi’s perspective which sheds a new light on the emotional depth and internal conflict of a woman who is historically framed by duty and drama. Similarly, Forest of Enchantments is the reimagining of Ramayana through Sita’s point of view. She is no longer a passive character but a resilient woman trying to navigate love, exile and agency. Divakaruni’s writing sheds light on the shift in India’s cultural narrative: a move away from grandeur for grandeur’s sake and towards a quieter layered way of storytelling that centres emotion and humanity. Her novels align perfectly with the visual and cultural transformation of today’s Indian design – minimal, introspective, yet rooted. Through her nuanced writing and restrained tone, she gives a fresh take on these epics without diminishing their gravitas. Her version of these tales does more than just giving voice to the sidelined heroines – they become influential, culturally rooted documents of the country rethinking power, gender and identity. Chitra Banerjee’s tales mirror the new onset of sensibility in India: less spectacle, more gravitas. And in that quiet, something truly powerful emerges.
